\begin{abstract}
We identify a structural correspondence between the analytic
combinatorics (AC) of Feynman integrals and the theory of deep
neural networks. The Kirchhoff polynomial of a neural network's
computational graph --- a generating function for spanning trees ---
controls the gradient flow, determines the edge-of-chaos
initialisation, and classifies phase transitions in learning.
The same tools that compute critical exponents in reaction-diffusion
field theory (Lagrange equations, Newton polygons, Hopf algebras)
apply to: (i)~the vanishing/exploding gradient transition in deep
networks; (ii)~the generalisation phase transition in overparameterised
models; (iii)~the loss landscape geometry of attention mechanisms.
We outline a research programme connecting these domains.
\end{abstract}

\section{The correspondence}

A neural network with $L$ layers defines a \textbf{computational graph}
$\Gamma$: a directed acyclic graph (DAG) where vertices are layers
(or neurons) and edges carry weight matrices $W_e$.  The network
function $f_\Gamma(x)$ is a composition of maps along paths in~$\Gamma$.

The Jacobian of the network with respect to its input is
\begin{equation}
J = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}
= \sum_{\text{paths } \pi} \prod_{e \in \pi} W_e \cdot \sigma'_e,
\end{equation}
where $\sigma'_e$ is the derivative of the activation function at
edge~$e$.  For gradient-based training, the key quantity is the
\textbf{expected squared Jacobian}:
\begin{equation}
\E\bigl[|J|^2\bigr]
= \sum_{\text{paths}} \prod_{e \in \text{path}} \E[w_e^2]
= \sum_{\text{paths}} \prod_e \alpha_e,
\label{eq:jacobian}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_e = \E[w_e^2 \cdot (\sigma'_e)^2]$ is the effective
edge weight.

This is a \textbf{path generating function} on the computational
graph~$\Gamma$.  For a layered network (chain graph), it reduces to
$\prod_{l=1}^L \alpha_l$ (product of per-layer weights).  For
networks with skip connections (residual networks, transformers),
it is a sum over multiple paths of varying lengths.

\begin{proposition}[Graph polynomial structure]
For a DAG $\Gamma$ with $V$ vertices and $E$ edges:
\begin{equation}
\E\bigl[|J|^2\bigr] = \Phi_\Gamma(\alpha),
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_\Gamma$ is a polynomial in the edge weights $\alpha_e$,
homogeneous of degree equal to the longest path length.  For
layered networks, $\Phi$ reduces to a product.  For networks with
skip connections, $\Phi$ is a sum of monomials corresponding to
distinct input-output paths.
\end{proposition}

The Kirchhoff polynomial $\K_\Gamma$ of the computational graph
and the path polynomial $\Phi_\Gamma$ are related: $\K$ counts
spanning trees while $\Phi$ counts paths.  For DAGs, these are
connected through the matrix-tree theorem applied to the
\emph{path Laplacian}.

\section{The vanishing/exploding gradient transition}
\label{sec:gradient}

\subsection{The phase transition}

A deep network with $L$ layers and per-layer weight variance
$\alpha$ has $\E[|J|^2] = \alpha^L$.  The gradient flow transitions
at $\alpha = 1$:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\alpha < 1$: $\E[|J|^2] \to 0$ exponentially (vanishing gradients).
\item $\alpha > 1$: $\E[|J|^2] \to \infty$ exponentially (exploding gradients).
\item $\alpha = 1$: $\E[|J|^2] = 1$ (edge of chaos, critical initialisation).
\end{itemize}

This is an \textbf{absorbing-state phase transition}: the gradient
signal either dies (absorbing state) or explodes (active state).
The critical point $\alpha = 1$ is the analogue of $R_0 = 1$ in
epidemiology or $\sigma = \lambda$ in reaction-diffusion.

\subsection{AC classification}

The generating function for the gradient signal in a depth-$L$
network is
\begin{equation}
G(z) = \sum_{L=0}^{\infty} \alpha^L z^L = \frac{1}{1 - \alpha z}.
\label{eq:gradient_gf}
\end{equation}

This is a \textbf{simple pole} at $z = 1/\alpha$.  The AC
classification:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\alpha < 1$: pole at $z = 1/\alpha > 1$ (outside unit disk,
  coefficients decay exponentially).
\item $\alpha > 1$: pole at $z = 1/\alpha < 1$ (inside unit disk,
  coefficients grow exponentially).
\item $\alpha = 1$: pole at $z = 1$ (on the unit circle, marginal).
\end{itemize}

At $\alpha = 1$, the singularity is a \emph{simple pole}, not a
branch point.  This means the gradient transition is
\textbf{first-order} (discontinuous), not second-order (continuous)
--- the gradient signal goes from decaying to growing with no
critical scaling in between.

\subsection{Residual networks: branch points from skip connections}

For residual networks (ResNets), the computational graph has skip
connections: each layer's output is $x_{l+1} = x_l + f_l(x_l)$.
The path polynomial becomes
\begin{equation}
\Phi_{\text{ResNet}}(\alpha, z) = \sum_{\text{paths}} \alpha^{|\text{path}|}
z^L = \prod_{l=1}^L (1 + \alpha_l z).
\end{equation}

For uniform $\alpha_l = \alpha$: $\Phi = (1 + \alpha z)^L$.
The generating function in $L$ is
\begin{equation}
G(z, u) = \sum_{L=0}^{\infty} (1 + \alpha z)^L u^L
= \frac{1}{1 - u(1 + \alpha z)}.
\end{equation}

At the critical depth $u = 1/(1 + \alpha z)$: this is again a
simple pole.  But if we consider the \emph{distribution} of
gradient signal across paths (not just the mean), the generating
function involves $\log(1 + \alpha z)$ --- which has a
\textbf{logarithmic branch point} at $z = -1/\alpha$.

The AC classification of the singularity determines the
\textbf{concentration properties} of the gradient:
\begin{itemize}
\item Simple pole: gradient dominated by one path (chain networks).
\item Logarithmic branch: gradient distributed across exponentially
  many paths (ResNets) --- this is why ResNets train more stably.
\item Square-root branch: gradient has power-law fluctuations
  (possible for attention mechanisms with variable-length paths).
\end{itemize}

\section{The generalisation phase transition}
\label{sec:generalisation}

\subsection{Overparameterisation as a Lagrange equation}

In the lazy training regime (neural tangent kernel), a network
with $N$ parameters and $P$ data points has an effective kernel
$K(x, x') = \sum_{i=1}^N \nabla_\theta f(x) \cdot \nabla_\theta f(x')$.
The eigenvalue distribution of this kernel undergoes a
\textbf{phase transition} at $N/P = 1$:
\begin{itemize}
\item $N < P$ (underparameterised): the kernel has a gap,
  training error is nonzero.
\item $N > P$ (overparameterised): the kernel is full-rank,
  training error is zero (interpolation).
\item $N = P$: the transition, with divergent condition number.
\end{itemize}

The resolvent of the kernel, $G(z) = \text{tr}(K - zI)^{-1}/N$,
satisfies a \textbf{self-consistent equation}:
\begin{equation}
G(z) = \frac{1}{z - \gamma \cdot \phi(G(z))},
\label{eq:resolvent}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma = N/P$ is the overparameterisation ratio and $\phi$
encodes the weight distribution.  This IS a Lagrange equation
$T = z \cdot \psi(T)$.

The singularity of $G(z)$ at the spectral edge determines:
\begin{itemize}
\item The Marchenko--Pastur distribution (square-root branch
  at the spectral edge, Puiseux exponent $1/2$).
\item The condition number scaling near the transition.
\item The generalisation error in the proportional limit $N, P \to \infty$
  with $\gamma = N/P$ fixed.
\end{itemize}

The AC framework classifies this singularity: the Newton polygon
of the resolvent equation determines whether the spectral edge is
a square root (generic), a cube root (at a tricritical point), or
a higher-order singularity (at special parameter values).

\subsection{Network architecture as graph topology}

Different architectures give different graph topologies $\Gamma$,
which give different Kirchhoff polynomials $\K_\Gamma$, which give
different singularity types for the resolvent.  The AC prediction:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Architecture} & \textbf{$\K$ structure} & \textbf{Singularity} \\
\midrule
Fully connected (chain) & $\K$ linear & simple pole \\
ResNet (skip connections) & $\K$ factorises & product of poles \\
Transformer (attention) & $\K$ non-factorisable & branch point \\
Random graph network & $\K$ from random graph & varies with $\gamma$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\section{Attention as a non-equilibrium field theory}
\label{sec:attention}

The attention mechanism in transformers computes:
\begin{equation}
\text{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V.
\end{equation}

The softmax creates a \textbf{probability distribution over tokens}
--- a stochastic process on the sequence.  The multi-head attention
with $H$ heads and $L$ layers creates a \textbf{random walk on a
graph} where:
\begin{itemize}
\item Vertices are token positions.
\item Edge weights are attention scores (stochastic: rows sum to 1).
\item Depth $L$ is the number of steps.
\end{itemize}

This is a \textbf{Markov chain on a random graph} --- exactly the
setting where the spectral dimension $d_s$ and the AC framework
apply.  The ``volume explored'' by the attention walk is the
effective context length: how many tokens genuinely influence the
output.

The \textbf{critical attention} hypothesis: the most effective
transformers operate near the phase transition where the attention
walk is critical (branching ratio $\approx 1$).  Too focused
(subcritical): the model attends to too few tokens.  Too diffuse
(supercritical): the model wastes capacity on irrelevant tokens.

At criticality: the attention-volume scales as
$V(L) \sim L^{d_s/2}$ where $d_s$ is the spectral dimension of the
attention graph.  The AC framework gives $d_s$ from the attention
matrix spectrum, and the scaling exponents from the Puiseux exponent
of the Lagrange equation for the attention walk.

\section{Concrete research programme}
\label{sec:programme}

\subsection{Immediate (computable now)}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Kirchhoff polynomial of ResNet architectures.}
Compute $\K$ for standard architectures (ResNet-50, DenseNet,
U-Net) and classify the gradient-flow singularity.  Predict
which architectures have stable gradient flow (factorisable $\K$)
vs unstable (non-factorisable $\K$).

\item \textbf{Spectral dimension of attention graphs.}
Given a trained transformer's attention matrices, compute $d_s$
of the attention graph.  Test the ``critical attention'' hypothesis:
is $d_s \approx 2$ (the marginal value for 2D random walks) for
well-trained models?

\item \textbf{Marchenko--Pastur singularity via AC.}
Classify the spectral-edge singularity of the neural tangent
kernel for different architectures using the Newton polygon of
the resolvent equation.  Predict generalisation transitions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Medium-term (requires development)}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, start=4]
\item \textbf{Loss landscape as a generating function.}
The partition function of a Bayesian neural network,
$Z(\beta) = \int e^{-\beta \mathcal{L}(\theta)} d\theta$,
is a generating function whose singularity in $\beta$ (inverse
temperature) determines the learning phase transition.
Apply AC to classify this singularity for specific architectures.

\item \textbf{Pruning as a percolation transition.}
Network pruning (removing weights below a threshold) is bond
percolation on the computational graph.  The percolation threshold
and cluster-size exponent are determined by the Kirchhoff polynomial
of the pruned graph --- exactly the network percolation analysis
from our earlier work, now applied to artificial (not biological)
networks.

\item \textbf{Hopfield networks and associative memory.}
The storage capacity transition ($\alpha_c = P/N$) of Hopfield
networks is a Lagrange equation whose singularity determines
whether retrieval succeeds.  Apply AC to modern Hopfield networks
(with polynomial or exponential interaction functions) to predict
capacity scaling.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Long-term (speculative)}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, start=7]
\item \textbf{Renormalisation group for deep learning.}
The depth of a network is analogous to the RG scale.  Each layer
``integrates out'' fine-grained features, producing an effective
theory at the next scale.  The RG $\beta$-function of this flow
determines the fixed points (optimal architectures) and the
universality classes (which architectures learn the same features).
AC provides the tools to compute this $\beta$-function from the
network graph.

\item \textbf{Scaling laws from AC.}
The empirical scaling laws of large language models
($L \sim N^{-\alpha}$ where $L$ is loss and $N$ is parameter count)
may be power laws from a branch-point singularity of a generating
function. If the training dynamics are described by a Lagrange
equation, the scaling exponent $\alpha$ is the Puiseux exponent
--- computable from the Newton polygon.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

The AC framework developed for reaction-diffusion field theory
--- Lagrange equations, Kirchhoff polynomials, Newton polygons,
Hopf algebras --- applies directly to neural networks when the
computational graph is treated as a physical graph.  The gradient
flow is a path generating function, the generalisation transition
is a Lagrange-equation singularity, and the attention mechanism
is a random walk whose spectral dimension determines the effective
context.

The most immediately testable prediction: the singularity type of
the gradient-flow generating function, determined by the Kirchhoff
polynomial of the computational graph, classifies which
architectures can be trained stably (factorisable $\K$, simple
poles) and which exhibit critical phenomena (non-factorisable $\K$,
branch points).
